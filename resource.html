RESOURCES OF OUR PLANET


RENEVABLE RESOURCES






Solar Energy:
Geothermal energy taps into the Earth‚Äôs internal heat, usually from hot water reservoirs or magma beneath the surface. This heat is used to produce steam that turns turbines and generates electricity. Geothermal power plants have a small land footprint and emit very low greenhouse gases, making them one of the cleanest and most efficient renewable energy options available.


Wind Energy:
Wind energy is generated by capturing the kinetic energy of wind using wind turbines. When the blades spin, they turn a generator that produces electricity. Wind farms can be built on land or offshore and are among the fastest-growing energy sources in the world. Wind energy is clean, efficient, and plays a significant role in reducing carbon emissions and combating global warming.



Hydropower Energy:
Hydropower uses the movement of water‚Äîtypically from rivers or dams‚Äîto generate electricity. As water flows through turbines, it spins them, producing power. It‚Äôs one of the oldest and most reliable forms of renewable energy. While large-scale dams can affect ecosystems, modern hydropower projects aim to balance energy production with environmental protection.


Biomass Energy:
Biomass energy comes from organic materials like crop waste, wood, animal manure, and even algae. When burned or converted into fuel, it can produce heat, electricity, or even transport fuels like bioethanol and biodiesel. Biomass helps reduce waste and is a carbon-neutral source if harvested sustainably, making it a practical solution for rural and agricultural areas.



NONRENEVABLE RESOURCES






Coal Energy:
Coal is a black, rock-like fossil fuel formed from ancient plant matter buried under the Earth‚Äôs surface for millions of years. It is primarily used to generate electricity in thermal power plants. However, burning coal releases high levels of carbon dioxide, sulfur dioxide, and toxic metals, which contribute to air pollution, acid rain, and climate change. It is one of the most environmentally damaging energy sources.


Oil Energy:
Oil, or crude petroleum, is a thick liquid fossil fuel found deep underground. It is refined into fuels like petrol, diesel, and jet fuel, powering most of the world‚Äôs transportation. Oil extraction and spills can damage marine ecosystems, and its combustion contributes heavily to greenhouse gas emissions, making it a major factor in global warming.



Natural Gas Energy:
Natural gas is a cleaner-burning fossil fuel made primarily of methane. It is used in cooking, heating, and generating electricity. While it emits less CO‚ÇÇ than coal and oil, it is still a non-renewable resource. Methane leaks during extraction and transportation are especially harmful, as methane is a far more potent greenhouse gas than carbon dioxide.


Nuclear Energy:
Nuclear energy is produced using uranium, a rare radioactive metal mined from the Earth. It generates large amounts of electricity through nuclear fission without releasing carbon dioxide. However, the radioactive waste it produces remains dangerous for thousands of years, and nuclear accidents can have catastrophic consequences for people and the environment.



WATER RESOURCES






Fresh Water:
Freshwater is essential for all life on Earth. It comes from sources like rivers, lakes, and underground aquifers. We use it for drinking, farming, sanitation, and industry. However, only about 2.5% of Earth‚Äôs water is freshwater, and much of it is locked in ice caps and glaciers. Pollution, overuse, and climate change are putting pressure on this limited supply. Protecting freshwater sources is crucial for human survival and ecosystem health.


Rainwater:
Rainwater is a renewable and clean source of freshwater. When collected and stored properly, it can be used for agriculture, household needs, and even drinking after purification. Harvesting rainwater helps reduce pressure on groundwater and municipal water systems, especially in regions facing drought or water scarcity.



Oceans and Seas:
Although oceans hold 97% of Earth‚Äôs water, they are salty and require desalination to be used for drinking. Oceans also support marine biodiversity, regulate the climate, and serve as a source of food and minerals. But they are under threat from plastic pollution, oil spills, and rising temperatures. Protecting marine ecosystems ensures the health of the planet and the people who rely on them.


Hydropower Energy:
Flowing water from rivers or dams can be used to produce electricity ‚Äî known as hydropower. It is one of the oldest and most reliable forms of renewable energy. Though cleaner than fossil fuels, large dams can disrupt ecosystems and displace communities. Responsible development of hydropower is key to balancing energy needs with environmental protection.
